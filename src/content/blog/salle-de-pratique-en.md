## The arch that falls

A wooden arch 6 metres high by 11 metres wide. We lift it with a mini Manitou that’s clearly not designed for this. It tilts. It falls.

Silence.

We all look around. No one underneath. No one hurt. No major damage. We breathe.

And we carry on.

That’s the Shala’s story. A crazy adventure, carried by a community, with moments when we almost broke everything — literally.

## The meeting

December 2022. Eliott Meunier is coaching me as part of an integral coaching programme he runs one-to-one. We don’t really know each other yet. Just exchanges, sessions, an intellectual connection.

April 2023. Eliott arrives at LAOM. We set up his Tiny House on the land. It’s official: he’s joining the adventure.

Once the Tiny is in place, we step out onto the terrace. We look ahead — a view of the hills and trees. He asks me:

— What have you got planned here, Charly?

— Here, I’ve planned a room. And some tipis.

He turns to me. His eyes light up.

— Wow. Creating a dance studio is my dream. What are you missing?

— Honestly? Money.

— OK. I have money. When do we start?

I look at him. I don’t hesitate for a second.

— Now.

## From idea to timber in 15 days

I call Serge Lièvremont right away. Serge is a former timber-frame house builder. He can draw, he can build, he knows materials.

We work on the moodboard together with Eliott. The vision takes shape: a 121 m² room, wooden arches, a generous volume, natural light. We sign off. We send it to Serge.

24 hours later, he sends back the plans.

We sign off again. I give him my timber merchant contacts. I say: “Do the cutting list and order the wood.”

15 days later, the wood is on site.

It’s July 2023. The build can start.

From the terrace conversation to the first hammer blow: under a month. That’s how we work.

## The community bet

We have the wood. We have the plans. We don’t have the hands.

Eliott has built a community of thousands. On our side, Amandine and Claire have created Amawé, a community of thousands around integrative health.

The idea is simple: what if we mobilised these communities to build together?

We put out the call. The response exceeds us.

Dozens of people want to come. Not just lend a hand for an afternoon. Really come. Stay. Build.

We set up a system: rotations of 20 people every 10 days. For 3 months, a steady flow of volunteers arriving, building, leaving, and passing the baton.

## A self-run mini-society

Very quickly we realise we’re no longer running a build. We’re running a micro-community.

30 people to feed at all times. The kitchen was a mess. Shopping meant 2–3 trolleys every time — €1000 a week. That means: organising living spaces, rotation schedules, non-stop washing up. Everyone pitches in. No one runs it alone.

But people don’t just come to build. They come to connect. For Eliott. To experience something different.

So we add self-organised activities. Volunteers suggest workshops, discussions, sharing sessions. Eliott and I improvise coaching — on business, on life, on investment, on living.

In the evening we eat together. We talk. We change the world. And the next day we pick up the tools again.

## The rough patches

I’ll be honest: we didn’t know what we were doing.

Eliott and I were running the build. But “running” is a big word. We were figuring it out day by day. Sometimes we gave instructions like we knew what we were doing — when we were improvising completely.

One day it was my first time on a Manitou. I mixed up the fork lift lever with the accelerator. Result: I drove into the building. I bent the bottom belt of the structure.

We fixed it. We carried on.

Summer in Aveyron is 35°C in the shade. We had to adapt: work sessions early morning, long break in the afternoon, back on site late in the day. Some days were hard. Fatigue built up.

And there was this constant pressure: people from Eliott’s community wanted time with him. They wanted activities, connection, value. We had to be site managers AND facilitators AND coaches AND hosts.

### The first argument

During this build we also had our first real argument with Eliott.

We had only just met. We were building together. We were living together. The pressure was rising. And one day it blew.

Eliott said to me: “Charly, you’ve gone down in my esteem.”

That’s hard to hear. Especially when you’re giving everything you’ve got on a build. Especially when you’re exhausted.

But that argument mattered. We had to really talk. Do some emotional regulation. Reconnect as humans, not as co-builders of a project.

It’s easy to show the build photos. It’s harder to show the moments when you wonder if you’ll keep working together.

We carried on. And we’re stronger today because of that argument. (Honestly, I don’t know — maybe I really did go down in his esteem and he’s been pretending ever since. Eliott, if you’re reading this, tell me!!)

## Beyond the build

The Shala taught us something we hadn’t anticipated.

We already had a taste for passing things on. But it was on this build that we discovered the power of the group. Not just coaching one person — but creating a space where 20 people meet, see each other, support each other. Where everyone brings something to the others.

The improvised coaching sessions were that. Not one-to-one in an office. Group conversations around a fire after a day on site. People asking questions about business, about life, about how we got here. And others answering, sharing their own experience.

That’s when we understood: the real magic is the collective. 20 people arriving, self-organising, building together — that’s something no budget can buy.

## What the Shala unlocked

Today the Shala - the practice room - exists. 121 m² of floor, light, space. No mirrors. No bars. Just a generous volume, living materials, and the view over the hills of the Grands Causses.

### The finishes

A year after the main structure, in April 2024, we move on to finishes. I work warm earth plasters with pigments extracted from our local soil.

Hands in the material. Colour emerging from the mix. The gesture repeating, refining, finding its rhythm.

And something shifts.

It’s no longer construction. It’s art.

That revelation stayed with me. Ecological building and artistic creation aren’t two separate things. A well-made plaster holds the same beauty as a canvas. That’s what I call the Fractal of Beauty: beauty at every scale, from the tiniest detail to the big picture.

### The place comes alive

The room breathes. Earth-plaster walls regulate humidity naturally. The air is healthy. Light comes in through large openings. The space invites you to move, dare, and let yourself be surprised.

This is where we host movement workshops. Dance sessions. Collective practice. [The “En Mouvement” workshop with Samuel Planas](/en/stage-danse/) — our signature immersion — exists thanks to this room.

Before the Shala, LAOM was a build site. After the Shala, LAOM became a place to live. This room made it possible to start running activities, welcome groups, and bring the ecolieu to life.

## What we learned

**On the power of the collective**: An engaged community can achieve what no budget can. But it takes organisation, humility, and a lot of relational energy.

**On what works**: 10-day rotations keep energy high while giving time to get involved. Self-run organisation (kitchen schedule, activities proposed by participants) frees up the organisers.

**On what doesn’t work**: Building without professionals means taking risks. We were lucky. The arch that fell, the Manitou into the building — it could have ended badly.

**On relationships**: Working together isn’t enough. You also need to know how to talk when things aren’t right. Our first argument with Eliott made us stronger.

**On ourselves**: We discovered we loved passing things on. That our stories had value. That coaching wasn’t a job we’d chosen — it was something that had found us.

---

The Shala isn’t just a building. It’s proof that when a community decides to create together, the impossible becomes possible.

And sometimes an arch has to fall before you really understand what you’re building.
